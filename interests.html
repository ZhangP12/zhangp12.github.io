Favorite Mathematical Problem: Does P = NP?

The P versus NP problem is probably the most important problem in theoretical computer science, and ranks among the top in all areas of mathematics. It is one of the seven Millennium Problems, with one million US dollars given to the person(s) who solves one of those problems. To date, only the Poincar√© conjecture has been solved.
Informally, the P versus NP problem asks whether every problem whose solution that can be quickly verified can also be solved quickly.

A Longer Explanation

The P versus NP problem concerns how quickly an algorithm can solve a problem given input n. P stands for polynomial time, while NP stands for nondeterministic polynomial time. Basically, P problems require an algorithm that at worst will require x^k time, where k is an integer. However, NP-hard problems require an algorithm that requires x! time or k^x time, so it grows exponentially. To clear up confusion, P problems are actually part of NP, because NP problems can be verified in polynomial time, and so can P problems. NP-hard problems are the hard ones (duh). If every problem which has a quickly verifiable solution also has a quick method of solving it (i.e. P=NP) then modern crytography would not work. Because cryptography relies on hard problems, P=NP would mean that most existing crytographic systems would be broken. However, most computer scientists believe that P does not equal NP.
